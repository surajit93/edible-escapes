The Hungry Compass – Chapter 1

Four Friends. Infinite Miles. Countless Meals.

Raj leaned back on the bamboo recliner, wiped his brow, and muttered,

"Hot. Too hot. Like a bloody tandoor. Bro, why are we here again?"

Arko, lying belly-down on the porch floor drawing mountain outlines in his notebook, chuckled,

"It’s called the art of suffering in silence. You city types wouldn’t understand."

He flicked a pebble toward a chicken, which fluffed up and fled indignantly.

"No AC, no waterfall, no breeze, no WiFi. You call this vacation?"

Raj grumbled, sipping warm coconut water straight from the nut.

Sayan emerged from inside, holding a rusted bicycle bell. "This thing still works. Found it near the papaya tree."

Raj frowned. "We’ve been in this village for five days. I have mosquito tattoos on my back."

"I heard about a place in Meghalaya," said Ron, who had just returned with two boiled eggs, a sketchpad, and a half-drunk Frooti. "Cleanest village in Asia. Mawlynnong. Bamboo bridges. Pork curry. Local rice beer."

Raj sat upright. "Did you say beer?"

Sayan’s eyes twitched with excitement. "Did he say pork?"

Ron smirked. "I also heard about a waterfall nearby where mist looks like it’s dancing."

Raj jumped. "I’m sold. Let’s bounce. Jeep?"

"Or hitchhike," Arko said.

"Last time I sat on a sack of onions for four hours," Raj groaned.

"You still smell like onion sweat," Sayan teased, dodging Raj’s flip-flop.

They packed like men on a quest, not tourists:

  • A tattered map with scribbled notes
  • One steel kettle
  • Arko’s tiny Bluetooth speaker
  • Ron’s flea-market camera
  • Sayan’s audio recorder
  • A torn novel
  • Two loaves of stale bread

The road to Mawlynnong was greener than a spinach smoothie. Every now and then someone would yell,

"Stop!"

They’d jump out of the slow-moving tempo to take photos of trees that looked like dancers or dogs that looked like philosophers.

By late afternoon, they rolled into the village. It smelled like turmeric, bamboo, and wet earth. Children ran past with bamboo hoops. Women sat on porches weaving baskets under mango trees.

A smoky shack lured them in. A Khasi woman stirred something heavenly.

She served them smoked pork with mustard leaves, bamboo shoot chutney, and wild black rice.

"This. This is religion," Raj whispered after a bite.

"This is heaven with chili flakes," said Sayan, mouth full.

That night, they pitched a tent beside a stream. Ron lit a fire, Arko played jazz, and Raj fell asleep hugging a bottle of rice beer.

At dawn, they hiked to the living root bridge. Roots twisted across a stream like ancient snakes. Mist floated over mossy stones like soft ghosts.

On the other side, a man roasted meat over open flame.

"Jadoh with pork liver,"

he said, handing out skewers. Also rice, steamed inside bamboo tubes.

The flavors were wild and spicy. The man waved away cameras. "Only taste. No photos."

They didn’t check into hotels. Instead, they asked a shopkeeper if they could sleep on his granary roof. "Only if you clean my courtyard," he said.

"Deal," Sayan replied instantly.

At 6 AM, fog hung like thick soup. They cleaned leaves, laughed, and debated who snores loudest.

Ron scratched his stubble. "Where next?"

"Nongriat," Arko said. "Hidden waterfall. Locals make something called kiat – a fermented brew from fruit skins."

Raj asked, "Can we swim?"

"Always," Sayan grinned.

On the trail, a child invited them to meet her grandmother who sings for guests. In a dim hut, an elderly woman tapped a tin plate and sang of clouds falling in love with rocks. Ron recorded it for their blog.

Later, they cooked rice and roasted jackfruit seeds with mustard oil and salt.

"This is better than Goa," Raj whispered under the stars.

"No crowd. No noise. No exes," Arko muttered. "Only pork and poetry."

They started talking about the next leg—Vietnam.

"Beer so fresh, it’s brewed in front of you," said Sayan.

"Pho. Hot. Smoky. Like soul soup," added Ron.

"Floating markets!" Arko chimed in.

"We need a name," Ron said.

"‘Fork Nomads’?" Arko offered. "‘Broth & Boots’? No, wait... ‘Hungry Compass,’" Sayan declared.

They all nodded. The journey had just begun.

Four friends. No maps. Just mouths to feed and stories to tell.
